## Q1. What work do you currently do?

I run two things. One is a Chai Point franchise outlet near Infopark, Kochi - that's the tech park area, so lot of IT crowd. I opened it in 2022. Second, I run a small tutoring centre for Class 10 and 12 students - maths and physics. I used to be a teacher at a CBSE school before all this. I quit teaching in 2021 because the pay was ₹28,000 a month and I had two kids. It wasn't enough.

### Q1 (a) Last time you considered going back to a job?

Last monsoon season. Business was terrible - rain in Kochi means nobody walks to a chai shop, everybody stays inside. My revenue dropped 40% for two months. I was thinking maybe I should just take a teaching job at one of these international schools, they pay ₹50,000-60,000. But then Diwali season picked up and things normalized.

### Q1 (b) What stopped you?

Freedom. When you've been your own boss for three years, going back to reporting to a principal and following a timetable feels impossible. Also my wife said "we've survived worse months, don't panic." She was right. We had enough in the emergency fund to cover two months of low income.

## Q3. Quick summary about your childhood.

Thrissur, proper middle-class Nair family. Achan was a clerk at the District Collectorate. Amma was a nurse at a government hospital. Money was okay - not rich, not poor. Kerala middle-class is different from North India middle-class. Even in a modest family, education is treated like oxygen. I was expected to be either a doctor or an engineer. I became neither. I did BSc Physics from Kerala University and then BEd. My parents were disappointed but they came around when I started teaching.

### One thing from childhood that affects decisions?

My father's government job mentality. He retired with a pension, PF, gratuity. Very secure. I think that's why I always keep an emergency fund even though I'm a business owner now. I have 6 months of expenses sitting in an SBI FD. My friends who run businesses think I'm being paranoid but I've seen what happens when you don't have a buffer - one of my neighbours lost his shop and had nothing to fall back on.

## Q5. Marriage and financial decisions.

Married Lakshmi in 2016. She's a nurse at Aster Medcity. She earns about ₹35,000 a month. Our finances are almost fully merged. We have a joint account at Federal Bank where both salaries go. We decide big purchases together - actually she decides most things. I handle investments, she handles monthly budgeting. It works because she's more disciplined than me.

### One decision that would've gone differently?

The Chai Point franchise. It cost ₹8 lakh to set up. If I was single, I might have gone for something riskier - maybe a full restaurant. But with two kids and a home loan, Lakshmi said franchise is safer because you get brand support, supply chain, training. She was right. The failure rate for independent restaurants is insane.

## Q7. Monthly spending breakdown?

Home loan EMI is ₹14,000 - we bought a flat in Kakkanad. Groceries about ₹10,000 - rice, coconut oil, fish, we buy from the local market mostly. Kids' school fees averaged out is ₹5,000. Transport - I have a Maruti Swift, petrol is maybe ₹4,000. Lakshmi takes the Kochi Metro to work. Tutoring centre rent is ₹6,000 - that's a business expense but it comes from the same pocket. SIPs ₹5,000 - two funds on ET Money app. Amma's medicines about ₹2,000. LIC premium ₹3,000. Random stuff maybe ₹3,000-4,000.

### One thing you'd never cut?

Kids' education expenses. Rithik is in Class 3 and already doing well in maths. If he wants tuition, coaching, books - whatever it takes. In Kerala if your child doesn't do well academically, the social pressure is unbearable. Every uncle and aunty will ask about marks.

## Q8. Savings and investments?

SBI FD for emergency - about ₹3.5 lakh. Two SIPs on ET Money. One LIC endowment policy - I know the returns are bad, Lakshmi's cousin who works at Axis Mutual Fund keeps telling me to surrender it, but I've paid 8 years of premium, only 7 left, so I'll just finish it. Gold - Lakshmi has about 40 grams, mostly from the wedding. We buy 2-3 grams every Onam and Vishu from Kalyan Jewellers. Chit fund - I'm in one local chit fund, ₹5,000 a month, 20 members. It's very common in Kerala.

### What would you never invest in?

Crypto. I saw a colleague lose ₹2 lakh in 2022. Also, I don't trust anything I can't physically see or understand the fundamentals of. I need to know where my money is sitting. Even with mutual funds I had to be convinced - the chit fund feels more real to me because I know the 19 other people in it.

## Q12. Religion and daily decisions?

5/10. We're Nair Hindu. Amma does puja every morning - we have a small puja room with a lamp. I don't do it daily but I join on Fridays and festival days. We go to Guruvayur temple once a year without fail - that's the big one for our family. Vishu, Onam, Thiruvathira - we celebrate everything properly. Amma insists on checking muhurat for anything major. When we bought the flat, we consulted an astrologer for the griha pravesham date. I don't fully believe in it but it makes Amma happy and it's not hurting anyone.

## Q17. Governance - agree or disagree?

3 out of 5. Neutral. Kerala has excellent healthcare and literacy - that's state governance, not central. Central government I have mixed feelings. GST simplified some things for my business but the compliance burden is still high for small business owners. I voted in both state and national elections. In Kerala, politics is very active - everyone has an opinion. I lean left but I vote based on the local candidate, not the party. Last election I voted LDF for state and UDF for national. Different reasons for each.

### One thing you'd change?

Make it easier to run a small business. The number of licenses, inspections, and compliance requirements to run a chai shop is absurd. I spent three months just getting the FSSAI license. Ease of doing business should mean ease for the ₹8 lakh franchise owner, not just for Reliance and Tata.
